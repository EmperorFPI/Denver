Powers of attorney before surgery. Medical directives in the ICU family room. A deed that has to be signed before hospice. These calls are urgent, emotional, and — this is the part nobody says out loud — they fail more often than any other kind of notarization, because the legal requirements don't bend for hard circumstances.

We'd rather prepare you properly than dispatch fast and fail at the bedside.

## What has to be true for a bedside notarization

Nevada law requires the same things at a hospital bed as at a kitchen table. The signer must be **awake and aware** — able to understand what the document is and communicate a willingness to sign. The signer needs **valid photo ID**, or a **credible witness** who personally knows them and has their own valid ID. And the document must be **complete, with no blanks**, before the notary arrives.

Sedation, heavy pain medication, and advancing dementia are the three most common reasons bedside notarizations fail. A notary cannot lawfully notarize for a signer who can't demonstrate awareness — and a notarization taken from an unaware signer is exactly what gets challenged in court later, which defeats the document's entire purpose.

## How we make these visits succeed

- **We ask the hard questions first.** Is the signer lucid right now? What times of day are they most alert? Is ID physically at the hospital? Five minutes on the phone prevents most failed visits.
- **We time the visit to the patient**, not the notary — early morning before medication rounds is often best.
- **We coordinate witnesses when the document needs them.** Many POAs and directives require witnesses in addition to the notary, and hospital staff often cannot serve. Arrange witnesses before the appointment.
- **We handle families gently and documents strictly.** Those aren't in tension — the strictness is what makes the document stand up later.
