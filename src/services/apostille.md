Apostilles are where small notarization mistakes become expensive. The Colorado Secretary of State will reject an apostille request over a wrong certificate, an expired commission, or an illegible seal — and you find out after you've mailed it, paid for it, and lost a week your visa timeline didn't have.

## What an apostille is, in plain terms

Foreign authorities can't verify a Colorado notary's commission on their own. The apostille solves this: the Colorado Secretary of State certifies that the notary (or state official) who executed your document was genuinely commissioned at the time. Under the Hague Apostille Convention, that single certificate makes your document acceptable in over 120 member countries without further embassy legalization.

## Documents that commonly need apostilles

- **Powers of attorney** for property or family matters abroad
- **Business documents** — corporate resolutions, agreements, distributor authorizations
- **Personal declarations and affidavits** — single-status affidavits for foreign marriage are a classic
- **School records and diplomas** for study or work visas (these often need specific handling — call first)
- **Adoption paperwork** for international adoptions

**Vital records are different.** Birth, death, and marriage certificates aren't notarized — the state apostilles the registrar's certified copy directly. If that's your document, you don't need a notary at all, and we'll tell you so instead of booking you.
