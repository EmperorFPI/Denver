  - q: "What ID is accepted for notarization in Nevada?"
    a: "A current, unexpired government-issued photo ID: driver's license, state ID card, U.S. or foreign passport, or military ID. If the signer has no valid ID, Nevada permits identification through a credible witness — someone who personally knows the signer and presents their own valid ID.   - q: "Can I sign my document before the notary arrives?"
    a: "For most notarial acts, no — the signature must be made in the notary's presence, and a jurat always requires signing in front of the notary. Bring the document complete but unsigned.   - q: "Can a notary notarize for a family member?"
    a: "A Nevada notary cannot notarize their own signature or any document in which they have a disqualifying interest. Notarizing for relatives is legally risky territory and best practice is simply not to — which is a genuine reason to use an independent mobile notary for family POAs and estate documents: independence is part of what makes the document defensible later."
  - q: "Does a notarization make a document legal or valid?"
    a: "No — this is the most misunderstood thing about notarization. The seal verifies who signed and that they did so willingly; it says nothing about whether the document is well-drafted, enforceable, or right for your situation. A notarized document can still be legally worthless if it was written badly. Document content is attorney work, and any notary who reviews or corrects your document's substance is practicing law without a license."
  - q: "What documents need witnesses in addition to the notary?"
    a: "Many Nevada estate documents — wills most prominently — require two witnesses beyond the notary, and witnesses generally can't be beneficiaries. Some medical directives and POAs have witness requirements too, depending on how they're drafted. Check your document's signature block before the appointment: if it has lines for witnesses, arrange qualified people in advance, because the notary usually cannot double as one."
  - q: "Can a notary certify a copy of my birth certificate or diploma?"
    a: "Vital records — birth, death, marriage certificates — cannot be copy-certified by a Nevada notary; certified copies come from the state registrar or the issuing agency. For other documents, Nevada permits notaries to certify copies in some circumstances, and schools often must certify their own records for apostille purposes. Tell us the document and the destination and we'll route you correctly, even when the answer is 'not us.'"
  - q: "What happens if the signer doesn't seem mentally aware?"

This comes up most at hospitals and memory-care facilities, and it's why we screen bedside visits by phone first. A notarization taken from an unaware signer is precisely what gets voided in court later, which defeats the document's purpose."

## The three rules that prevent failed appointments

Nearly every failed notarization traces to one of three things, so they're worth stating before the questions:

1. **Valid, unexpired photo ID** — for every signer, physically present at the appointment.
2. **Don't pre-sign** — signatures generally happen in the notary's presence.
3. **Complete documents, no blanks** — a notary cannot notarize a document with empty spaces, and cannot fill them in for you.

Get those three right and the appointment is fifteen boring minutes, which is exactly what a notarization should be.
